2020 has really been a tough year.

The COVID-19 pandemic has completely changed the way of living as we all know. Wearing a mask, back-to-back zoom meetings, work from home, virtual birthday parties, socially distanced hangouts, and so on. Adjusting to the new “normal” was definitely a frustrating experience for most of us.



“... Remote learning proved disastrous for many in the spring, as schools were forced to devise learning plans on the fly and struggled to connect students with devices and high-speed Internet. Experts fear it could be another factor exacerbating an achievement gap that separates Black and Latino students from their White classmates.”
Reference: Washington Post



The pandemic has also caused a large disruption to the education system, affecting 94% of the student population in the world. Distance learning tools like radio, television, paper-based learning, and online learning are adopted to continue the education while practicing social distancing. But distance learning showed limitations since many students were not equipped with devices or had access to high-speed internet.



As the daily new case numbers gets lower, the schools are considering reopening for full or partial in-person education. But some of the schools are remaining remote-only. White students and students of color aren’t equally likely to live in a remote-only district. Learning loss will probably be greatest among low-income, black, and hispanic students as they are less likely to have access to high-quality remote learning as seen from these charts.
Reference: McKinsey
